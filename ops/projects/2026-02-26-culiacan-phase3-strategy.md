## A1. Mitigation Hierarchy Matrix

The mitigation hierarchy is applied to each of the 10 ecological risks identified in Phase 2 (B4). The hierarchy follows the BBOP (Business and Biodiversity Offsets Programme) / IFC Performance Standard 6 sequence: **Avoid** impacts first, then **Minimize** what cannot be avoided, then **Restore** degraded conditions, and only then **Offset** any residual impact.

**Context note**: This project is unusual in that the baseline is an ecological desert (>90% impervious, NDVI 0.03). The mitigation hierarchy applies primarily to *construction-phase impacts* and *operational risks*, not to existing ecological values — there are almost none to protect. The project IS the restoration step.

## B1. NbS Intervention 1: Green Corridor Network

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Solution** | Linear Urban Green Corridors — native canopy tree planting with bioretention understory along reclaimed street lanes |
| **Target Risks** | R-ECO-1 (zero baseline), R-ECO-2 (tree mortality), R-ECO-4 (monoculture), R-ECO-5 (invasives), R-ECO-10 (climate change) |
| **Area** | 15–25 km of corridor × 6–12m width = **9–30 ha of new green infrastructure** |
| **Location** | Phase 1: Álvaro Obregón, Ángel Flores, Juárez/Rosales, Aquiles Serdán (Priority Index top 10%). Phase 2–3: Secondary streets radiating from core. |
| **Species Palette** | **Canopy layer (6,000–10,000 trees)**: *Tabebuia rosea* (Amapa rosa, 30m, spreading canopy, mass-bloom pollinator resource — IUCN LC), *Guazuma ulmifolia* (Guásima, 30m, wide open canopy, fruiting for avifauna — IUCN LC), *Prosopis* spp. (Mezquite, 5–15m, deep taproot, extreme drought tolerance — IUCN LC most spp.). **Understory/ground cover**: *Bouteloua gracilis* (Blue grama grass — native Sinaloa), *Muhlenbergia rigida* (Purple muhly — native), *Ruellia nudiflora* (native wildflower, pollinator). **No single species >40% of canopy total.** |

| **Water System** | Drip irrigation from cisterna pluvial (§5.2). Hydrogel root-zone amendment (0.5 kg/tree pit). Bioretention swales between tree pits capture and infiltrate street runoff. |
| **Implementation Timeline** | Year 1: Corridor 1 (Álvaro Obregón) — 3.5 km pilot. Year 2: Corridors 2–3 (Ángel Flores, Juárez/Rosales). Year 3: Corridor 4 (Aquiles Serdán) + secondary network. Planting timed to June–September wet season (Culiacán monsoon). Site prep (demolition, utility relocation, structural soil installation) during October–May dry season. |
| **Maintenance** | Tree care (structural pruning Years 1–5; maintenance pruning Years 5+): certified arborist, annual. Irrigation management: cisterna system, automated drip controllers. Pest/disease monitoring: quarterly IPM inspection. Invasive removal: annual survey + 48-hour response. Root monitoring: GPR biennial scan. Estimated cost: USD 40K/km/year (ref: project roadmap §3.2). |
| **Expected Ecological Outcomes** | Canopy cover: <5% → 35–50% along corridors (10-year target). Mean NDVI: 0.04 → 0.15+ within corridor footprint (5-year target). Surface temperature: 2–5°C LST reduction along corridors (10-year target; benchmark: Medellín achieved 2°C city-wide with 30 corridors — Alcaldía de Medellín, 2019). Native species richness: <5 → 15+ species/km. Pollinator presence: from near-zero to measurable (bee/butterfly transect surveys). |
| **Credit Potential** | **Carbon**: Eligible under Verra VCS or Mexico Forest Protocol v3.0 (Large Urban Forestry). Estimated 500–2,000 tCO₂e/year from 6,000–10,000 trees (conservative allometric, 30-year horizon). **Biodiversity**: Eligible under Verra Nature Framework / SD VISta (open to all projects globally since Jan 2026). Urban biodiversity uplift from near-zero baseline — strong additionality case. |
| **Precedent** | Medellín Green Corridors (30 corridors, 2°C UHI reduction — C40 CFF-supported); Barcelona Superblocks (urban greening + traffic reduction); Singapore Streetscape Greenery Master Plan (300 km of green corridors); Curitiba Green Exchange (community-driven urban forestry). |

## B2. NbS Intervention 2: Cisterna Pluvial Integrated Water Management

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Solution** | Rainwater harvesting systems integrated into parking hub structural design — subsurface cisterns feeding gravity-drip irrigation to corridor network |
| **Target Risks** | R-ECO-2 (tree mortality — water stress), R-ECO-7 (flood risk), R-ECO-10 (climate change — drought amplification) |
| **Area** | 6,000–25,000 m² rooftop collection surface (3–5 hubs × 2,000–5,000 m²/hub) |
| **Location** | Co-located with each automated parking hub. Distribution radius: 500m per hub (covers adjacent corridor segments). |
| **System Specification** | **Collection**: Hub rooftops + adjacent reclaimed parking surface. **First-flush diverter**: 2mm initial rainfall diverted to stormwater (removes pollutants). **Filtration**: Mesh screen + sand filter pre-cistern. **Storage**: Subsurface reinforced concrete cisterns (500–2,000 m³/hub), integrated into hub foundation — zero additional land footprint. **Distribution**: Low-pressure drip irrigation to corridor tree pits within 500m radius. Automated soil moisture sensors trigger irrigation. **Overflow**: Excess directed to bioretention swales in corridor network. |
| **Capture Volume** | Annual: 683 mm × collection area × 0.85 efficiency = **3,500–14,500 m³/year** total across all hubs. Dry-season demand: ≈15 L/tree/week × 2,000 trees/hub zone × 32 dry weeks = **960 m³/hub/season** — within cistern capacity. |
| **Implementation Timeline** | Concurrent with hub construction (Year 2–3). Cisterns are a structural element — designed and poured with foundations, not retrofitted. Irrigation network installed with corridor planting prep. |
| **Maintenance** | Annual cistern inspection and sediment removal. Filter replacement: biannual. Sensor calibration: annual. Pump servicing (if needed): within DBOM scope (§5.4). Estimated marginal OPEX: 3–5% of hub operating budget. |
| **Expected Ecological Outcomes** | Tree survival rate: >95% through establishment (vs. <70% without irrigation in BS1(h')W conditions). Dry-season canopy maintenance: trees maintain partial leaf cover through drought, accelerating canopy closure timeline. Stormwater infiltration: 3,500–14,500 m³/year removed from surface runoff — reducing flash flood peak by estimated 15–25% within corridor catchment. |
| **Credit Potential** | **Carbon**: Indirect — cisterns enable the tree planting that generates carbon credits. Not directly creditable. **Water**: Potential eligibility under Gold Standard Water Benefit Certificate if water balance impact is quantified. **Adaptation**: Contributes to CFF Pillar 2 adaptation narrative — integrated water-sensitive design. |
| **Precedent** | Singapore ABC (Active, Beautiful, Clean) Waters Programme (urban rainwater harvesting at infrastructure scale); Medellín EPM Water Management (integrated NbS water systems); Mexico City Isla Urbana (30,000+ rooftop rainwater systems in semi-arid urban context — demonstrated feasibility in Mexican regulatory environment). |

## B3. NbS Intervention 3: Bioretention Street Corridors

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Solution** | Distributed bioretention cells and permeable surface systems within green corridor cross-section — stormwater treatment + infiltration + urban cooling |
| **Target Risks** | R-ECO-7 (flood risk), R-ECO-8 (downstream RAMSAR water quality), R-ECO-1 (zero baseline — soil ecosystem restoration) |
| **Area** | Integrated within corridor footprint: bioretention swales occupy 20–30% of corridor cross-section width (1.5–3.6m of 6–12m corridor). Total bioretention area: **1.8–9.0 ha** across 15–25 km network. |
| **Location** | Between tree pits in every corridor segment. Sized for 25mm rainfall capture (95th percentile storm event for Culiacán). |
| **System Specification** | **Cross-section** (top to bottom): (1) Native perennial ground cover (*Bouteloua gracilis*, *Muhlenbergia rigida*) — 150mm. (2) Bioretention soil media (60% sand, 20% compost, 20% native topsoil) — 600mm. (3) Choking layer (pea gravel) — 50mm. (4) Aggregate drainage layer — 200mm. (5) Underdrain (perforated PVC) connecting to municipal storm sewer with upturned elbow (retains first 150mm of storage). **Kerb cuts** allow street runoff to flow into bioretention cells. **Overflow**: To storm sewer via underdrain when capacity exceeded. |
| **Implementation Timeline** | Installed concurrently with corridor construction (utility relocation → structural soil → bioretention cells → tree planting → surface finishing). Phase 1 (Year 1): Álvaro Obregón pilot — 3.5 km. |
| **Maintenance** | Sediment removal: biannual (after each monsoon season). Vegetation maintenance: quarterly cut-back of native grasses. Media replacement: every 15–20 years (or when infiltration rate drops below 25 mm/hr). Estimated cost: included in USD 40K/km/year corridor maintenance budget. |
| **Expected Ecological Outcomes** | Stormwater capture: 25mm design storm retained = 450–2,250 m³ per storm event across network. Annual volume managed: estimated 10,000–50,000 m³/year (depending on final network length). Pollutant removal: 80–90% TSS, 50–70% metals, 40–60% nutrients (benchmark: US EPA bioretention performance data). Soil biota restoration: bioretention media creates 1.8–9.0 ha of biologically active soil where only concrete existed — supports soil microbiome, invertebrates, and mycorrhizal networks that sustain tree health. |
| **Credit Potential** | **Carbon**: Soil organic carbon accumulation in bioretention media — small but measurable (estimated 1–3 tC/ha/year in bioretention soil media — USDA NRCS). **Water**: Quantifiable stormwater benefit for potential Gold Standard Water Benefit Certificate. |
| **Precedent** | Philadelphia Green City Clean Waters (1.5 billion gallons/year stormwater captured through distributed green infrastructure — largest US municipal NbS stormwater program); Portland Green Streets (bioswales in 2,000+ street locations); Melbourne Water Sensitive Urban Design (WSUD) standards. |

| **Expected Ecological Outcomes** | Habitat connectivity: from zero (completely severed) to functional grid linking 2 river systems through urban core. Species movement: corridor designed to permit dispersal of *I. iguana* (NOM-059 Pr), foraging flights of *L. yerbabuenae* (NOM-059 A) and *A. finschi* (NOM-059 P). Urban avifauna: breeding bird richness target of 15+ species within corridor network within 5 years (benchmark: Singapore green corridors support 30+ bird species in comparable urban context — Sodhi et al., 2011). |
| **Credit Potential** | **Biodiversity**: Strong additionality — connecting previously isolated urban habitat to riparian zones. Eligible under Verra Nature Framework. Quantifiable through species richness and connectivity index metrics. |
| **Precedent** | Singapore Nature Ways (ecological connectivity corridors between parks and nature reserves through urban streets); Medellín Parques del Río (river-to-city green connectivity); London All of Lights (bat-friendly corridor lighting design — Bat Conservation Trust UK). |
